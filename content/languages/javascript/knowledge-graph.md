# {{ Technology }}

## Definition

> **Learning Goal**
>
> Help the reader understand **why this technology exists**.
>
> Explain:
>
> - The problem it was created to solve.
> - A brief historical context (only if relevant).
> - Why that problem mattered.
> - How the technology addressed it.
>
> Do not explain how the technology works internally.
>
> Do not explain where it is used.
>
> Do not explain advantages or disadvantages.
>
> The reader should naturally understand what the technology is without explicitly defining it.

---

## How it Works

> **Learning Goal**
>
> Help the reader understand **how the technology solves the problem described above**.
>
> Before writing:
>
> 1. Build a Concept Graph.
> 2. Order concepts from the most fundamental to the most advanced.
> 3. Introduce one concept at a time.
>
> Rules:
>
> - Every paragraph introduces exactly one new concept.
> - Every new concept is immediately defined.
> - Never use a concept before defining it.
> - Avoid implementation details that belong in the Deep Dive.
> - Avoid transition phrases such as:
>
>   - "To understand..."
>   - "Now..."
>   - "Next..."
>   - "Let's..."
>   - "Before..."
>
> Let the structure guide the reader.

## How it Fits into the Ecosystem

> **Learning Goal**
>
> Show where this technology fits inside real software systems.
>
> Explain:
>
> - Its role.
> - The technologies that commonly surround it.
> - How they interact.
>
> Before introducing each concept, classify it:
>
> - **Core Concept**
>   Explain briefly.
>
> - **Context Concept**
>   Provide only the context needed.
>
> - **Knowledge Base Concept**
>   Provide minimal context and highlight it using **bold**.
>   Replace it with an internal link once that document exists.
>
> The objective is to help readers visualize complete production systems.

## Real-World Usage

>
> Cover:

> - When it should not be used.

> - Disadvantages.
> - Trade-offs.
> - Common alternatives.

## Practical Examples

> **Learning Goal**
>
> Demonstrate how the technology is applied in real production environments.
>
> Prefer:
>
> - Software architectures.
> - System diagrams.
> - Production workflows.
> - Real engineering scenarios.
>
> Avoid examples whose only purpose is demonstrating syntax.
>
> The reader should immediately recognize where this technology appears in a real software system.

# Writing Checklist

Before considering the document complete, verify that:

- Every section answers only its own learning objective.
- Every paragraph introduces, defines or expands exactly one concept.
- No important concept is assumed.
- No unnecessary transition sentences are used.
- The reader can naturally infer the most important conclusions without a "Key Takeaways" section.
- The document builds a mental model rather than a collection of facts.
- The content has high knowledge density: every sentence adds new understanding.
